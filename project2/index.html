Project 2

Deceptive Visualization?

Marco Curiel — m1curiel@ucsd.edu

Proposition: The NYPD’s policing reforms, including the rollout of body-worn cameras in 2019, gave investigators more evidence to confirm incidents, which led to more substantiated complaints.

For the Proposition

Figure 1: Substantiated misconduct cases rose after body cameras became common, suggesting increased accountability.

Design Decisions and Rationale

  • Cherry picking timeframes: I only showed 2009–2010 and 2019–2020, skipping the years in between to make the jump look cleaner and more dramatic. By hiding those middle years, the increase seems steady and significant, even if the real trend isn’t. I did this to tell a simple “before vs. after” story that people can quickly understand. It makes the chart persuasive because the change looks clear and purposeful. It worked well visually, but it oversimplified the data. I first thought of including all years since that made the graph busier and less focused.
  • Emphasizing percentage change (+11.2 pts):I used a big red arrow, bold font, and the label “+11.2 pts” to make the difference stand out. The bright color and large text pull attention to the increase and make it feel bigger and more important than it might be. I did this because people naturally focus on large, colorful numbers, and it makes the story easy to remember. It makes the chart persuasive because the change looks impressive and hard to ignore. It worked well for grabbing attention, but it leaves out important context like sample size or uncertainty. I first tried smaller text and neutral colors, but that made the visual lose its impact.
  • Causal framing through title and layout:The title “Body Cameras Shed Light on Misconduct” and the upward arrow make it seem like body cameras directly caused the increase in substantiated complaints. The layout also reads like a story and pushes the idea of progress and improvement. I did this to make the chart feel more direct and convincing, since viewers can quickly connect cause and effect. It makes the message strong and easy to follow, but it also implies causation that the data doesn’t actually prove. I first thought of using a neutral title, but the causal one made the story more memorable and attention-grabbing.
Against the Proposition

Figure 2: After body cam rollout, substantiated cases dropped while exonerations climbed, showing a shift in complaint outcomes.

Design Decisions and Rationale

  • Selective reference line:I added a dashed line labeled “Body Cam rollout begins (Apr 2017)” to make it look like that moment caused the shift in complaints. The line acts as a visual anchor, so people connect the trend change to that event. It made the story feel cleaner and more intentional, but it also hid that the trend started earlier. I thought about removing the line or adding more context, but leaving it made the message stronger and easier to understand in a quick view.
  • Scale and normalization: I used raw counts instead of rates to make the changes look bigger and more dramatic. Showing totals exaggerates the movement between categories, even if the overall number of cases dropped. This helped the graph feel more impactful and aligned with the “shift” story. It worked visually, but it oversimplified the data. I considered using percentages, but it made the differences smaller and less convincing.
  • Emotional color cues: I used red for “substantiated ↓” and green for “exonerated ↑” to trigger emotional reactions. People naturally see red as bad and green as good, so the colors helped tell the story without extra text. It made the chart more persuasive and memorable and it also created a bias. I tested softer colors but decided to keep the strong ones because they draw immediate attention and supported the narrative.

Final Reflection

Working on both charts showed me how much power design choices have over how people read data. In the first chart, the “for” version, I only used two time periods (2009–2010 and 2019–2020) to make the rise in substantiated complaints look simple and dramatic. Skipping the years in between made the jump look clean, even though the real data had a lot more variation. I also highlighted “+11.2 pts” in bold red so it grabbed attention right away and made the change feel more important than it really was. It was easy to code in Python, but it was harder to decide what to show and what to hide. That part made me realize how easy it is to shape a story without ever technically lying. The second chart, the “against” version, showed the full data from 2005 to 2019, including Substantiated, Unsubstantiated, and Exonerated outcomes. I added a shaded area for 2017 to show when body cams rolled out, but that choice also pulled attention toward one specific point in time and made people connect that moment to the shift in trends. Color played a big role too, red made the “substantiated” line feel bad, and green made “exonerated” feel positive, even though those labels are neutral in reality. This version gave a clearer picture, but it was harder to keep clean and visually balanced without losing focus. I think ethical visualization is about being clear without being misleading. You can guide attention, but you shouldn’t bend the story or remove context just to make a point. The first chart crossed that line a bit because it hid too much, while the second one stayed fair even though it looked messier. What worked best was seeing how small details, like colors, titles, and scaling, completely change how someone interprets data. What didn’t work was how easily those same details can push people toward a biased takeaway. Now, whenever I look at a “nice” chart, I’ll try to ask what’s missing or why it looks that way, because sometimes what’s left out says more than what’s shown.
